# Apparel Rubric

For t-shirt graphics, streetwear, print design, merch, and apparel assets.

Apparel is judged differently from screen work: it has to survive a physical medium, read across a room, and sit on a body that a buyer chose to identify with. Production readiness and commercial strength carry the most weight here.

## Weighted categories

| Category | Weight | What it measures |
|----------|:------:|------------------|
| Production readiness | 20% | Will it print cleanly and survive the chosen process and garment? |
| Typography | 15% | Is the type intentional, legible, and authoritative — not default? |
| Commercial strength | 15% | Would the target buyer wear it and pay for it? |
| Originality | 15% | Is it distinctive, or a generic AI / template tee? |
| Composition | 10% | Is the layout balanced and considered for placement and scale? |
| Brand fit | 10% | Does it match the brand's voice and buyer identity? |
| Clarity | 10% | Does the concept read instantly? |
| Color logic | 5% | Is the palette coherent and printable? |

Weights sum to 100%. Category scores are 0–10; the weighted total maps to the 0–100 taste score.

## Production rules

Treat these as hard checks. Several can trigger a `reject` override on their own.

- **Avoid unreadable small type.** Minimum legible size on a worn garment is larger than on screen. Flag any text that disappears at arm's length.
- **Watch thin linework.** Strokes under ~2pt fill in, break up, or vanish on cotton — especially screen print. Flag hairlines.
- **Flag excessive detail.** Dense gradients, fine textures, and tiny elements degrade in print and read as noise from distance.
- **Flag too many colors for screen printing.** Each color is a screen and a cost. Flag anything beyond ~4–6 spot colors unless DTG/DTF is intended.
- **Flag weak contrast on garment color.** Evaluate the design *against the actual shirt color*, not a white canvas. Low contrast kills it.
- **Consider DTG vs screen print.** DTG handles gradients and photoreal but fades and sits softer; screen print is punchy and durable but color-limited. State which the design assumes.
- **Consider print placement and scale.** Center chest, left chest, full front, back, sleeve — each has a size and a reading distance. Verify the design fits its placement.
- **Consider the 6–10 foot test.** Apparel is seen across a room before it is seen up close. If the concept doesn't read at distance, it fails.
- **Consider buyer identity fit.** A tee is a statement the wearer makes about themselves. Ask whether the concept fits *who the buyer wants to be seen as* — not just whether it looks nice.

## Reviewer notes

- Separate concept from craft. A great idea rendered in default type with hairline detail is a `revise`, not a `reject`.
- "Looks cool on a white mockup" is not the bar. The bar is "prints clean, reads at distance, and the target buyer wears it."
